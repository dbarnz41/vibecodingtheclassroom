VibeCoding
The Core Skill

The PICE Framework

The one skill that unlocks everything. Four letters. Four questions. Use them every time you write a prompt and you will never get slop again.

The four elements
P
Persona
Who should the AI be? Without this, it defaults to a dry corporate assistant. Be specific — name a voice, a role, a vibe.
I
Instructions
What exactly should it do? Number the steps if you need to. The AI follows instructions beautifully — if they're actually there.
C
Context
What's the situation? Year level, subject, student needs, time constraints, energy of the room. The AI can't read your classroom — tell it everything.
E
Example
Show it what good looks like. One concrete example beats 100 words of description. Always include this — it's the secret weapon.
See the difference
Vague vs. PICE

Same topic. Same AI. Completely different output.

Vague prompt
"Make me a quiz about photosynthesis."
What you get:
  • A generic 10-question quiz
  • No differentiation or year-level fit
  • Textbook-dry language
  • Needs heavy rewriting
PICE prompt
You are a Year 10 Biology teacher. Create a 5-question quiz on photosynthesis for students who struggle with abstract concepts. Use everyday analogies. Format: Q / A / 1-line explanation.
What you get:
  • Year-level appropriate language
  • Grounded in everyday analogies
  • Consistent, usable format
  • Ready to use immediately
Deep dive
Each element, unpacked

Click any letter to expand examples and tips.

P

Persona

Who should the AI be? ▼

The persona is the single most impactful line of your prompt. It changes the tone, vocabulary, authority level, and creativity of everything that follows. Without it, the AI defaults to a generic assistant voice — technically accurate but lifeless.

Tip: You can reference a real voice. "Write this like David Attenborough narrating a nature documentary" will produce different results to "You are a gritty crime noir author." Both are valid — the key is intentionality.

Adventure game example ▼
You are a gritty narrator writing a choose-your-own-adventure game for Year 11 Biology students, set in a fossil excavation in the Australian outback. Your tone is tense, cinematic, and occasionally darkly funny.
SAC feedback example ▼
You are an experienced VCE examiner who gives honest, specific, and constructive feedback. You speak directly to the student, not about them. You never use vague filler phrases like "good effort."
I

Instructions

What exactly should it do? ▼

This is where you spell out the mechanics. Number the steps. Use "exactly," "always," "never" when you have specific requirements. Don't leave room for interpretation where you don't want it.

Rule of thumb: If a substitute teacher couldn't follow your instructions alone, neither can the AI.

Adventure game example ▼
1. Write exactly 5 scenes with 3 choices each. 2. Each scene = scene title + 2-3 sentences of situation + choices A, B, C. 3. Mark the correct answer and explain why in 1 sentence. 4. Wrong choices lose points but never end the game. 5. End with a total score out of 100 and one reflection question.
C

Context

What's the situation? ▼

The AI has no idea what your classroom looks like, who your students are, or what's going on this term. You have to tell it. The more specific your context, the more tailored the output.

Don't forget: The "vibe" of the lesson is valid context. "Friday period 6 energy" will change the tone of output in ways that matter.

Context example ▼
Year 11 Biology, Unit 3 AOS 1 — cellular respiration. 30 students, mixed ability. Need to cover aerobic and anaerobic pathways. Game should take 15 minutes to play. Needs to be fun — Friday period 6 energy. Students respond well to humour and competition.
E

Example

Show it what good looks like. ▼

This is the secret weapon most teachers skip. One concrete example beats 100 words of description. Show the AI the exact format, structure, and style you want — it will replicate it precisely.

Pro tip: If you have a real example of something you like — a past question, a colleague's resource — paste it in. Say "write in the style of this example."

Scene format example ▼
Format each scene exactly like this: SCENE 1: THE OUTBACK DIG SITE You find an unusual bone protruding from the shale. The sun is brutal. Time is short. What do you do? A) Dig it out quickly before the light fades B) Photograph, tag, and catalogue before touching anything C) Call the site supervisor immediately → Correct: B — field protocol requires documentation first. Points: A = -10, B = +15, C = +5
Level up
Two power moves

Once you've got PICE, these two techniques take your prompting to the next level.

01

Meta-prompting

Ask the AI to ask YOU questions first.

The move ▼
Before you build anything, ask me 5 questions about my students, subject, and goals. Once I've answered, build the tool based on my answers.
02

Socratic prompting

Make the AI question, not answer.

The move ▼
Don't give the student the answer directly. Instead, ask them 3 guiding questions that help them reach the answer themselves. Only confirm or correct after their third attempt.

Ready-to-use sentence starters

Copy any of these directly into Gemini or Claude. Edit the bracketed parts for your context.

🎭 Persona starters
You are a [subject] teacher at a Victorian secondary school... You are an enthusiastic but exacting [role] who... You are a gritty narrator writing for [year level] students... You are an experienced VCE examiner who gives direct, specific feedback...
📋 Instruction starters
Write exactly [number] scenes / questions / tasks... Each [item] must include: [element 1], [element 2], [element 3]... Mark the correct answer and explain why in 1 sentence... Do not give the student the answer directly. Instead... End with a [score / summary / reflection question]...
🗺 Context starters
Year [X] [Subject], [Unit/AOS]. [Number] students, mixed ability... This is for a [15-minute / 1-period / homework] activity... Students respond well to [humour / competition / real-world examples]... The vibe should be [engaging / dramatic / low-stakes fun]... This aligns with VCE [Study Design reference]...
💡 Example starters
Format each [item] exactly like this example: [paste your example here] Write in the style of this sample I'm about to paste... Here's a question I like. Generate 5 more in this exact style: [paste question] Use this rubric language as your reference: [paste rubric]
🔁 Iteration prompts
That's close, but make it [harder / funnier / more dramatic]... Keep the format the same but change the topic to [X]... The language is too formal. Rewrite for a Year 9 reading level... Add a fourth option that's a plausible distractor... Rewrite scene 3 so the correct answer is less obvious...
🎯 Full PICE template
You are [PERSONA]. [INSTRUCTIONS — numbered steps for what to produce] Context: [Year level, subject, AOS, student needs, time available, desired tone] Format each [item] exactly like this: [EXAMPLE — paste or describe your ideal output format]
Scaling Up · Act 4

The GEM Workshop

Turn a one-off prompt into a reusable classroom tool. A Gem is a saved AI persona — write it once, upload new content each week, get consistent results every time.

What is a Gem?
Your prompt, made permanent.

A Gemini Gem is a saved AI persona that you return to week after week. Instead of rewriting your prompt for every new topic, you save it once. Then you just upload new content and let the Gem do the rest.

💡

Think of it this way

System prompt = the job description. Short, clear, defines the role.
Your uploaded notes = the week's raw material. New topic every time.
The Gem reads both and produces a finished, formatted classroom tool.

Step by step
Build your first Gem

These steps take about 5 minutes from scratch.

1

Go to gemini.google.com

In the left sidebar, look for "Gems." Click "New Gem" to open the Gem editor.

2

Name it and add your system prompt

Give the Gem a descriptive name (e.g. "Choose-Your-Adventure Generator"). Then paste your PICE prompt into the Instructions field.

→ Keep the system prompt under 200 words. Detail goes in the uploaded file, not the prompt.

3

Save the Gem

Click Save. The Gem now lives in your sidebar permanently — you can access it any time from any device logged into your Google account.

→ Your Gem is private by default. You can share a link with colleagues once it's working.

4

Use it — every week

Open the Gem. Upload your week's notes, a PDF chapter, a dot-point list, whatever you have. Type one line: "Make a [game/gameshow/tutor] from this content." Done.

The secret weapon
Don't write the Gem. Get AI to write it for you.

You already built a game you like. Use this master prompt to turn it into a Gem system prompt automatically.

The Master Prompt — paste into Gemini after your first build ▼
I just built a choose-your-own-adventure game with you. Now I want to turn it into a reusable Gem I can use every week for different topics. Write me a system prompt I can paste into a Gemini Gem. The Gem should: — Follow the same format as the game we just made — Use the same tone and scene structure — Generate a new adventure each time I upload new topic notes — Work for any subject or year level Keep it under 200 words. Use placeholders like [TOPIC] and [YEAR LEVEL] where the content will change each week.
✦ Meta-Prompts

Three more prompts that build your Gem for you

These work for any type of Gem — gameshow, SAC feedback, differentiation engine, Socratic tutor. Swap the tool name.

Prompt 1 · Build from scratch ▼
You are an expert in building Gemini Gems for classroom teachers. I want to create a Gem that generates [TYPE OF TOOL] from uploaded content. Before you write the system prompt, ask me 5 clarifying questions about: the students, the subject, the format I want, whether it needs to be reusable, and any style preferences. Once I answer, write a complete Gem system prompt under 200 words.
Prompt 2 · Adapt your existing prompt ▼
Here is a prompt I've been using: [PASTE YOUR EXISTING PICE PROMPT] Rewrite this as a Gemini Gem system prompt. It should: — Be under 200 words — Be reusable for any topic (use [TOPIC] as a placeholder) — Tell the Gem to read uploaded content and apply it to the format above — Keep my tone and style intact
Prompt 3 · Audit your Gem's output ▼
Look at the output you just generated. Now check it against the rules in my system prompt. Go through each rule one by one: — Did you follow the exact format I specified? — Is the tone consistent throughout? — Did you include all required elements? — Is there anything that doesn't match my instructions? List any rules you broke and rewrite those sections to fix them.
